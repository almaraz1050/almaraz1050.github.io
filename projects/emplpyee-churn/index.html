**Who is most likely to leave?** Churn is not randomly distributed across the workforce. The analysis found that risk is concentrated in specific groups defined by a combination of tenure, pay, education, and location. - Employees in the **mid pay tier** showed the highest churn, suggesting dissatisfaction is strongest when compensation feels neither entry-level nor rewarding enough to retain. - Employees who had been **benched** (unassigned to a project) churned at noticeably elevated rates, pointing to how periods of inactivity can signal instability or exclusion. - **Master's degree holders** churned at higher rates than both bachelor's and PhD employees, indicating that highly educated employees may feel underutilized or lack clear advancement paths. - **Pune** had the highest city-level churn among the three metros (Bangalore, Pune, New Delhi), suggesting that location-specific workplace factors play a role. - **Female employees** showed a higher churn proportion than male employees in the dataset. The tuned Decision Tree surfaced **joining year, compensation tier, and education level** as the top splits, showing that churn risk often emerges from the intersection of these factors rather than any single one acting alone.
**Actionable strategies for retention** The model results translate directly into targeted interventions HR teams can act on. - **Target high-risk segments.** Concentrate retention outreach on long-tenured, mid-pay employees, who emerged as the most at-risk combination across both models. - **Review compensation structure.** A pay equity analysis focused on the mid tier could address the dissatisfaction driving elevated churn in that group. - **Minimize benching impact.** Reducing bench time or improving communication and engagement during unassigned periods can lower the sense of job insecurity that leads to voluntary departure. - **Location-specific programs.** Pune employees warrant a dedicated retention initiative, as city-level effects suggest that workplace dynamics there differ from Bangalore and New Delhi. - **Career development for highly educated employees.** Creating clearer advancement tracks and recognition programs for employees with graduate degrees can reduce the gap between expectation and reality that drives churn in that group.
**Dataset and problem setup** The project used the *Employee Future Prediction* dataset from Kaggle (N = 4,653 employees across Bangalore, Pune, and New Delhi). The outcome variable indicates whether an employee left the company within two years. The dataset has a meaningful class imbalance: 65.6% of employees stayed while only 34.4% churned, which made recall for the churn class the primary evaluation metric throughout the project. **Models** Two supervised learning approaches were compared. *Logistic Regression* was estimated first as a baseline, then tuned with L1 regularization (lasso), class-weight balancing, and a regularization strength of C = 0.1 using GridSearchCV. L1 regularization shrinks weaker coefficients to zero, improving interpretability. The improved logistic model achieved a churn recall of approximately 0.63. *Decision Tree Classifier* was first run untuned and already outperformed the logistic baseline (recall 0.64). The pre-pruned version applied class_weight="balanced", max depth of 8, minimum 10 samples per leaf, and a lowered decision threshold of 0.35 to flag borderline cases as churners. This final model reached test-set recall of 0.79. **Validation** 5-fold cross-validation was used throughout to confirm that model performance generalized beyond the training split and was not sensitive to any particular partition.
**What this project demonstrates** - Churn is **highly predictable** when models are built with the right evaluation framework. Accuracy alone is misleading on imbalanced datasets; recall forces the model to actually catch at-risk employees. - **Different algorithms offer different kinds of understanding.** Logistic regression surfaces interpretable odds ratios, while decision trees reveal how multiple attributes interact to create risk pathways neither factor creates alone. - **Threshold tuning matters.** Lowering the decision threshold from 0.50 to 0.35 lifted recall from 0.64 to 0.79 on the decision tree without additional feature engineering, a practical lever that is often overlooked. - The analysis shows how employee data can move from descriptive patterns to an actionable early-warning system that HR departments can use before turnover occurs rather than after.
